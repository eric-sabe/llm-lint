The bus was late again, so I walked. Rain had been falling since morning and the
gutters were full. Two kids raced paper boats down the curb and lost both to a drain.
I bought coffee from the place on the corner, the one that never gets my name right,
and stood under the awning until the worst of it passed.

My grandfather fixed clocks. He kept the small tools in a cigar box and would not let
anyone touch them. When a watch came in he turned it over twice, listened to it, and
set it on the bench like a patient. Most of the time the problem was dust. Sometimes a
spring had given out and he cut a new one from wire, swearing softly the whole way.

We argued about money and about the dog and about whose turn it was to call the plumber.
None of it mattered much. The roof held. The garden came back every spring whether we
deserved it or not. I learned to bake bread badly and then a little less badly, and the
kitchen smelled like yeast on Sundays.

Years pass faster than you expect. The kids who raced boats have kids of their own now.
The coffee place changed hands twice and still gets my name wrong. I keep the cigar box
on a shelf where I can see it, though I never did learn to fix a watch.
